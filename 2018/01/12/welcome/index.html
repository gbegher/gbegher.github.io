Introduction

Posted in Introduction, Overview

Hello and welcome.

I am Gerrit Begher, a mathematician and software engineer living in Berlin.

The goal of this blog is to serve as a notepad for my research. It contains surveys, sketches and research notes about the connection between higher category theorety and programming languages. Some knowledge of category theory is required, of course. This post contains a roadmap of the upcoming topics I intend to write about. In the future, I will eventually extend it in order to provide additional explanations and framing for the individual posts.

Another project of mine is to build a functional reactive programming environment. This blog will also serve as a hub to explain the ideas behind this project. For now - Just a short sketch: One idea is to move away from a textual represenation. Instead, the AST is directly manipulated through an API with Git-like features. The syntax is based on nested arrow comprehension expressions. They are compiled into a nested DAG serving as a database schema. Every update to the AST can be used to derive a corresponding data migration. The approach is related to David Spivak’s OLOGs and Algebraic Databases.

I hope you enjoy visiting this blog and I am looking forward to get feedback. For now, there is no way to drop comments so feel free to send me an email.

Let’s start with the roadmap:

Arrowized programming

Arrows provide an abstraction for computation. We start out this section with a series of posts providing a survey.

  • From monads to monad comprehension and back again

    Survey: We give an introduction to monads on a cartesian monoidal category. We then introduce monad comprehension as a way to write programs. Finally, we arrive at the slogan “A monad is something that lets us interpret monad comprehensions”.

  • Arrows as an abstraction for computation

    Survey: Some notions of computation can not be modeled using monads. The more general concept of arrows provides an abstraction to deal with these shortcomings.

  • Form arrows to arrow comprehension and back again

    Survey: Just as for monads there is also a notion of comprehension for arrows. It allows for a very convenient way to compose computations with arrows. We repeat our discussion of monads and investigate the slogan “An arrow is something that lets us interpret arrow comprehensions”.

  • Digression: Generators in Javascript and arrow comprehension

    Survey: We imagine a better world where Javascript has pure functions - and thus cloneable generators - and investigate how comprehension can be related to generator functions.


The Grothendieck construction

The Grothendiek construction provides a way to capture the slogan “The whole is more than the sum of its parts”. It explains how individual parts can be glued together to form a larger object. Conversely, it also gives us a way to describe decompositions of objects. Our goal is to understand how the Grothendiek construction relates to arrow comprehension.

  • Grothendieck construction I a: Indexed direct sums of sets

    Survey: We give an introduction to indexed sums of sets and how they relate to partitions of a set.

  • Grothendieck construction I b: Set comprehension

    Survey: We take a close look at one of the most common syntactic elements of mathematical texts: Set comprehension.

  • Grothendieck construction II a: Presheaves and distributors

    Survey: We investigate on how to take into account additional structure on the indexing set - namely that of a category - and how to generalize this to families of sets indexed by two categories.

  • Grothendieck construction III a: Indexed and fibered categories

    Survey: After adding structure to the base as described in Part II a we now add structure to objects we index. Specifically, we define indexed categories and fibered categories and establish an equivalence between the two concepts.

  • Grothendieck construction II b: Enriched distributors

    Research notes: We give an explicit description of a generalization of the Grothendieck construction for presheaves and distributors to the setting of enriched categories.

  • Grothendieck construction III b: Decomposition of enriched categories

    Research notes: We describe a Grothendieck construction for enriched categories indexed by an enriched category and compare the construction to classical indexed categories. `

  • Grothendieck construction IV: Power graphs

    Survey: We investigate the relation between power graphs - a tool used to understand and visualize large graphs - and the Grothendieck construction.

  • Grothendieck construction V: Directed (acyclic) graphs

    Survey: We generalize the relation between power graphs and the Grothendieck construction to directed graphs.

  • Grothendieck construction VI: Comprehension and nested DAGs

    Research notes: We describe how comprehension expressions can be compiled into DAGs.
